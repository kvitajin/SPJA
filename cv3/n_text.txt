0 He was a Northern journalist, and it was in the interest of his paper
1 that he found himself, one bright March morning, in New Orleans, almost
2 dazed by the rapidity with which he had been whirled from the ice and
3 snow of the frozen North to the sunshine and flowers of the sunny South.
4 
5 He was charmed with the quaint and unique Crescent City. It was a
6 totally different world from that in which he had been reared--a summer
7 land, warm, indolent, luxurious, where one plucked the golden oranges
8 from the dark-green boughs, laden at once with flowers and fruit, and
9 where the senses were taken captive by the sensuous perfume of rare
10 flowers that, in his Northern land, grew only within the confines of
11 the close conservatory. Then, too, the dark, handsome faces of the
12 people, and their mixture of foreign tongues, had their own peculiar
13 charm. Nothing amused him so much as a stroll through the antique
14 French Market, with its lavish abundance of tropic vegetables, fruits,
15 and flowers, vended by hucksters of different nationalities in the
16 Babel of languages that charmed his ear with the languorous softness of
17 the Southern accent.
18 
19 He had a letter of introduction to a member of the Jockey Club, and
20 this famous organization at once adopted him, and, as he phrased it,
21 "put him through." The theaters, the carnival, the races, all whirled
22 past in a blaze of splendor never to be forgotten; for it was at the
23 famous Metairie Race-course that he first met Mme. Lorraine.
24 
25 But you must not think, reader, because I forgot to tell you his name
26 at first, that he is the Little Nobody of my story. He was not little
27 at all, but tall and exceedingly well-favored, and signed his name
28 Eliot Van Zandt.
